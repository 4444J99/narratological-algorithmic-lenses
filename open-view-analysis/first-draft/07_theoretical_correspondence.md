## II. Aristotelian Correspondence

**Source:** Aristotle, *Poetics* (~335 BCE)

### Application Assessment

| Principle | *Open View* Application | Assessment |
|-----------|------------------------|-------------|
| **Unity of Action** | Fragmented—parallel structure fragments single action | ⚠ Partial |
| **Unity of Time** | ~24-36 hours—compressed timeframe | ✓ Met |
| **Unity of Place** | Glass house primary, but extensive secondary locations | ⚠ Partial |
| **Probability & Necessity** | Black Box activation lacks causal trigger | ✗ Not Met |
| **Recognition (Anagnorisis)** | Li as collaborator—major recognition | ✓ Present but undeveloped |
| **Reversal (Peripeteia)** | Game announcement; test mechanics | ✓ Strong |
| **Catharsis** | Incomplete—script terminates before purgation | ✗ Absent |
| **Hamartia (Tragic Flaw)** | Family's dysfunction as collective flaw | ⚠ Implicit |

### Aristotelian Fit: ●●○○○

**Analysis:**

*Open View* operates in tension with Aristotelian principles. The fragmented structure (parallel betrayals; HQ subplot) violates unity of action. The arbitrary game activation violates probability and necessity. Most critically, the incomplete third act prevents catharsis—viewers cannot experience emotional purgation because the drama never resolves.

However, the script does achieve strong reversal (the game announcement transforms domestic drama into survival horror) and has potential for powerful recognition (Li's collaboration reframes her entire arc).

**Recommendation:**
If pursuing commercial viability, strengthen causal logic to satisfy "probability and necessity." If pursuing art cinema register, the Aristotelian violations may be intentional—but complete the third act to enable some form of catharsis, even if frustrated.

## III. McKee's Story Principles

**Source:** Robert McKee, *Story: Substance, Structure, Style and the Principles of Screenwriting* (1997)

### Application Assessment

| McKee Principle | *Open View* Application | Assessment |
|-----------------|------------------------|-------------|
| **Controlling Idea** | Present: surveillance = violence; family = already dead | ✓ |
| **Story vs. Plot** | Story = dysfunction; Plot = game | ✓ Distinct |
| **Gap between Expectation and Result** | Domestic drama becomes horror | ✓ Strong |
| **Progression of Complications** | Weak in parallel section; strong in siege | ⚠ Uneven |
| **Crisis-Climax-Resolution** | Crisis (Lef's death) → Climax (tests) → Resolution (ABSENT) | ✗ Incomplete |
| **Character Arc** | Li: victim → perpetrator (revelational) | ✓ Strong |
| **Consistent Antagonism** | HQ present but abstract | ⚠ Partial |
| **Dialogue as Action** | Strong in bathroom scene; weak in HQ scenes | ⚠ Uneven |

### McKee Fit: ●●●○○

**Key McKee Concern:**

McKee emphasizes that "story events must be causally connected" and that each scene should turn on a value. The parallel structure of Act II creates scenes that are internally coherent but externally disconnected—they proceed via AND THEN rather than BUT/THEREFORE.

**McKee's Inciting Incident Test:**

McKee argues the inciting incident should launch the central dramatic question. In *Open View*, two candidates exist:

1. **Line 222:** "We are getting divorced" (8% through script)
2. **Line 2415:** "Splayed, weighed, and displayed" (86% through script)

The divorce announcement functions as a false inciting incident—it launches the domestic drama but not the survival horror. The true inciting incident (game announcement) arrives at 86%, far later than McKee's 25% guideline.

**Recommendation:**
Either accept this as art cinema structure (delayed reveal that reframes prior material) or plant stronger hints of the game earlier to create anticipation rather than surprise.

## IV. The South Park But/Therefore Test

**Source:** Trey Parker and Matt Stone, commentary on *South Park* writing process

### The Principle

Every scene connection should be expressed as either:
- **BUT** (contradiction/reversal)
- **THEREFORE** (causation/consequence)

Never:
- **AND THEN** (mere sequence)

### *Open View* Application

| Transition | Current Connection | Assessment |
|------------|-------------------|------------|
| Prologue → Act I | THEREFORE (loops establish what Act I reveals) | ✓ |
| Divorce → Breakfast | AND THEN (scenes are parallel, not causal) | ✗ |
| Breakfast → Black Box | AND THEN (box arrives independently of family action) | ✗ |
| Black Box → Family Dispersal | AND THEN (no causal link) | ✗ |
| Li at McDonald's → Li's Affair | AND THEN (time sequence, not causation) | ✗ |
| Li's Affair → Li's Violence | THEREFORE (affair's degradation leads to violence) | ✓ |
| Parallel Threads → Convergence | AND THEN (time forces convergence) | ✗ |
| Convergence → Lef Crisis | AND THEN (crisis begins independently) | ✗ |
| Lef Crisis → Lockdown | THEREFORE (crisis triggers security) | ✓ |
| Lockdown → Lef Death | THEREFORE (no one can help) | ✓ |
| Lef Death → Black Box Mandate | THEREFORE (death triggers next phase) | ✓ |
| Mandate → Tests | THEREFORE (mandate requires tests) | ✓ |
| Tests → Li Reveal | AND THEN (weak causal link) | ✗ |

### But/Therefore Score: ~40% (Weak)

**Analysis:**

The script's parallel structure inherently creates AND THEN connections—each family member's day proceeds independently until evening forces convergence. The siege section (Lef's death through tests) achieves stronger causal binding.

**Recommendation:**
1. Add intersections between parallel threads (e.g., Rok glimpses Li with Def; Sus calls home during ballet)
2. Connect Black Box activation to family action (e.g., Li's violence triggers surveillance escalation)
3. Strengthen Li reveal connection to Rok's test

## V. Larry David's "Cascading Consequences"

**Source:** *Curb Your Enthusiasm* and *Seinfeld* structure analysis

### The Principle

Seemingly unrelated threads converge through cascading consequences. Each character's independent action creates ripples that collide in the finale.

### *Open View* Application

The script sets up cascading potential:
- Li's violence (stomping Def) could trigger surveillance response
- Fre's desperation (begging Nol) could make him vulnerable to manipulation
- Sus's abuse history could explain her test choice
- Rok's suppressed desire could predict his immediate button-press

**Current Status:** Threads are established but don't cascade. Each character's parallel story affects only themselves—no ripples reach other characters until the game forces convergence.

**Recommendation:**
Create at least one moment where Character A's action affects Character B before the game begins. Example: Sus's ballerina peers mention they saw Li at McDonald's with a man; this information reaches Fre, deepening his paranoia.

## IX. Debord and Spectacle Theory

**Source:** Guy Debord, *Society of the Spectacle* (1967)

### Correspondence

| Debord Concept | *Open View* Expression |
|----------------|------------------------|
| "The spectacle is not a collection of images, but a social relation among people, mediated by images" | Family communicates through devices; cannot connect without screens |
| "All that once was directly lived has become mere representation" | Family's dysfunction is revealed as always having been content |
| "The spectacle's function is the concrete manufacture of alienation" | Glass house literalizes alienation through visibility |
| "In a world which really is topsy-turvy, the true is a moment of the false" | The "game" reveals the true family; the "normal" life was performance |

### Extension

*Open View* extends Debord's 1967 critique into the surveillance capitalism era. Where Debord analyzed commodity culture and passive consumption, the script adds:
- Algorithmic observation (the house watches and responds)
- Unwitting participation (the family doesn't know they're content)
- Violence as entertainment (the game makes suffering productive)

**Thematic Validity:** ✓ Strong
